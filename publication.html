Adjournment.

[21 May, 1891.] Answer to Address in Reply. 189

Mr. A’BECKETT: As proposed in this bill?

Mr. WILLIS: The hon. member on that occasion sought to curry favour with the sturdy miners; but he comes here to-night with an effrontery which can only be exhibited by a follower of the Premier. He told us that the vote to-night was inopportune. I consider the electors of Peak Hill and the electors of the Bogan generally are to be congratulated on the unanimous vote which they passed that the hon. member had not their support and was not in touch with their interests. I had the honor of addressing the sturdy miners of Peak Hill, and a more intelligent and more noble body of electors I never addressed in my life, and I do not think any hon. member ever addressed a finer body of men. The hon. member comes here to-night, and he tells us, forsooth, that he will vote with the Premier; but he told the electors of Peak Hill that he is not in favour of this federation scheme. What kind of men are these to legislate for us, who go before their constituents and say one thing, but who, when the Premier shakes his stick, vote in the opposite way? I shall not pretend to go into this question to-night, as I think it would be premature. I have performed my duty to my constituents; I have done my duty to my country in voting against this proposition. I have no more to say, sir, than to thank you for the courtesy which you have shown to all members in the House. While I am here I shall try and obey the Chair, for I can congratulate the House and the country upon having an impartial Speaker, who at least deals out justice to all manner of men, and does not, as a late speaker did, deal out one kind of treatment to one class and another kind to another class.

Mr. SPEAKER: Order.

Mr. DIBBS: I shall be very much obliged to the hon. member at the head of the Government if he will, in his reply, inform the House if it is his intention to go in for a sitting to-morrow after the House has presented the address in reply to the Governor?

Sir HENRY PARKES: No!

Mr. DIBBS: I am very much obliged to the hon. member. I want to make my arrangements for the following week, and I desire to know whether or not he intends to proceed with his federation motion on the first Government sitting day next week?

Sir HENRY PARKES, in reply: The question which the hon. gentleman has put to me is, I think, a very reasonable one. I am obliged to say that the first thing which I shall consider it my duty to do will be to get the business of the House in proper order by adopting the sessional orders, and electing a chairman of committees. If the hon. member will, on Tuesday, put the question as to when we shall submit our motion on federation, I shall be prepared then to tell him. It is hardly reasonable to expect me to do that now, as the debate which has taken place has entirely disarranged the plans we had.

Question resolved in the affirmative.

House adjourned at 1·56 a.m. (Friday).

Legislative Assembly. Friday, 22 May, 1891.

Answer to Address in Reply.

Mr. SPEAKER took the chair.

ANSWER TO ADDRESS IN REPLY.

On motion by Sir Henry Parkes, the House proceeded to Government House, there to present to his Excellency their address in reply to the Governor’s opening speech.

The House being returned,

Mr. Speaker reported that the address in reply to the Governor’s opening speech had been presented, and that his Excellency had been pleased to make the following answer:—

Mr. Speaker, and Gentlemen of the Legislative Assembly: I have to thank you in her Majesty’s name for your loyal address in reply to the speech at the opening of the Parliament, and for the expressions of attachment to her throne and person contained therein.

Motion (by Sir HENRY PARKES) agreed to:

That the House do now adjourn until 4 o’clock on Tuesday next.

House adjourned at 4·5 p.m.
